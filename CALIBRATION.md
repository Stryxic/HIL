## Part I: Canonical Academic Text  
### Scientific Explanation and Lawfulness  
*(After Carl G. Hempel, Philosophy of Natural Science — calibrated excerpt)*

SECTION 1: SCIENTIFIC EXPLANATION

Scientific explanation is treated as a logical relation between statements rather than as a psychological or pragmatic act. An explanation consists of an explanandum, describing the phenomenon to be explained, and an explanans, consisting of general laws and specific conditions from which the explanandum can be derived. The central concern is not persuasion or understanding, but logical support.

Explanation is distinguished from mere description by the presence of general principles. A description may catalogue events, but an explanation subsumes those events under regularities that exhibit lawful connection. The explanatory force of a statement depends on its role within a structured system of laws and conditions.

The same logical structure underlies both explanation and prediction. If a set of premises is sufficient to explain an event after it occurs, it would also have been sufficient to predict that event before it occurred. This symmetry is a defining feature of scientific explanation.

SECTION 2: LAWS OF NATURE AND EXPLANATION

Laws of nature are not accidental generalizations but statements expressing invariant relations. They support counterfactual reasoning and play a central role in explanation. An explanatory account requires at least one law-like statement that connects the initial conditions to the phenomenon under consideration.

The distinction between law-like statements and accidental generalizations is critical. Only the former can function within explanatory arguments. The explanatory power of a theory depends on the extent to which it incorporates genuine laws rather than isolated observations.

Scientific explanations aim to reveal patterns of dependence. These patterns are not reducible to singular facts but require structured relationships expressed in general terms.

SECTION 3: THE DEDUCTIVE–NOMOLOGICAL MODEL

According to the deductive–nomological model, a scientific explanation takes the form of a valid deductive argument. The explanandum follows logically from the explanans, which includes at least one general law and a set of initial conditions.

For an explanation to be adequate, the premises must be true or well-confirmed, the argument must be logically valid, and the explanans must contain laws essential to the derivation. The explanatory relevance of the premises depends on their role in the deduction.

The deductive–nomological model provides a formal schema for understanding explanation, even though not all scientific explanations conform to it perfectly.

SECTION 4: REQUIREMENTS OF ADEQUACY

Not every valid deduction counts as an explanation. Explanatory adequacy requires that the laws cited be genuinely explanatory rather than incidental. The premises must contribute to the derivation in a non-redundant way.

An explanation that includes irrelevant conditions, even if deductively valid, fails to meet standards of adequacy. The concept of explanatory relevance is therefore essential to distinguishing genuine explanations from spurious ones.

These requirements introduce constraints that shape the structure of acceptable explanations without appealing to pragmatic or psychological considerations.

SECTION 5: INDUCTIVE–STATISTICAL EXPLANATION

Some scientific explanations are not deductive but probabilistic. In such cases, the explanans confers a high probability on the explanandum rather than entailing it with certainty. These explanations rely on statistical laws rather than universal ones.

The explanatory force of an inductive–statistical explanation depends on the strength of the probabilistic connection. Although the explanandum is not logically necessitated, it is rendered expectable given the explanans.

This form of explanation extends the general framework while preserving the emphasis on lawful connection and structural support.

SECTION 6: CONFIRMATION AND EVIDENCE

Scientific hypotheses are supported to varying degrees by evidence. Confirmation concerns the logical relations between evidence statements and hypotheses, not the psychological states of scientists.

The degree of confirmation depends on how strongly the evidence supports the hypothesis relative to alternatives. No finite set of observations can conclusively verify a universal law, but evidence can increase or decrease its credibility.

Confirmation theory aims to clarify these relations without reducing them to subjective belief or convention. The focus remains on structural relations among statements.
